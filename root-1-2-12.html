    Chapter 9 Sequences and Recurrence Relations

    In this chapter we will study sequences of numbers that are built recursively. Technically, a sequence (of real numbers) is a function \(a\) from \(\mathbb{N}\) to \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) If \(n\in\mathbb{N}\text{,}\) it is common to write \(a_n:= a(n)\text{.}\) We refer to \(a_n\) as the \(n\)th term of the sequence. We will abuse notation and associate a sequence with its list of outputs, namely:
    \begin{equation*} {(a_n)_{n=1}^{\infty}\:= (a_1,a_2,\ldots)}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which we may abbreviate as \({(a_n)}\text{.}\) Sometimes we may start our sequences at \(n=0\) as opposed to \(n=1\text{.}\) That is, we may allow the domain of a sequence to be \(\mathbb{N}\cup \{0\}\text{.}\)

    Define \(a:\mathbb{N}\to \mathbb{R}\) via \(a_n=\frac{1}{2^n}\text{.}\) Then we have
    \begin{equation*} a=\left(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{4},\ldots\right)=\left(\frac{1}{2^n}\right)_{n=1}^{\infty}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    It is important to point out that not every sequence has a description in terms of an algebraic formula. For example, we could form a sequence out of the digits to the right of the decimal in the decimal expansion of \(\pi\text{,}\) namely the \(n\)th term of the sequence is the \(n\)th digit to the right of the decimal. But then there is no nice algebraic formula for describing the \(n\)th term of this sequence.
    ğŸ”—
    Loosely speaking, a sequence of numbers is defined recursively if the \(n\)th term of the sequence is defined in terms of â€œearlierâ€ terms of the sequence. We have already encountered one famous example of a sequence being defined recursively, namely the Fibonacci sequence \((f_n)\text{,}\) which we defined by \(f_1=1\text{,}\) \(f_2=1\text{,}\) and \(f_n=f_{n-1}+f_{n-2}\) for \(n\geq 3\text{.}\) The equation \(f_n=f_{n-1}+f_{n-2}\) is the recurrence relation while \(f_1=1\) and \(f_2=1\) are the initial conditions. It is important to emphasize that we cannot define the Fibonacci number using only the recurrence relation since otherwise, we would not be able to â€œget startedâ€ with the recurrence.

      \begin{equation*} P(n,k)=P(n-1,k)+kP(n-1,k-1)\text{.} \end{equation*}

    By solving a recurrence relation together with its initial conditions we mean finding an explicit expression (sometimes called a closed form) for an arbitrary term \(a_n\) as a function of \(n\) (but no earlier terms of the sequence). The explicit expression for \(a_n\) is called the solution of the recurrence relation. For example, each time we found an explicit formula for the \(n\)th term of a sequence in the previous problem, we were solving the recurrence relation and the corresponding expression we found is the solution. By the general solution of a recurrence relation, we mean the set of its solutions given any initial conditions.

    A linear constant-coefficient recurrence relation of order \(r\) has the form
    \begin{equation*} {a_n=c_1a_{n-1}+c_2a_{n-2}+\cdots + c_ra_{n-r}+f(n),} \end{equation*}
    where \(c_1,c_2,\ldots,c_r\) are real numbers with \(c_r\neq 0\text{.}\) Such a recurrence relation is said to be homogeneous if \(f(n)=0\text{,}\) so that it can be written as
    \begin{equation*} {a_n=c_1a_{n-1}+c_2a_{n-2}+\cdots + c_ra_{n-r},} \end{equation*}
    and is non-homogeneous otherwise.
    ğŸ”—
    Notice that every arithmetic progression and every geometric progression is a first order linear constant-coefficient recurrence relation. In particular, each geometric progression is homogeneous while each arithmetic progression is non-homogeneous.

    If \(s_1(n),\ldots,s_k(n)\) are solutions to the linear constant-coefficient homogeneous recurrence relation
